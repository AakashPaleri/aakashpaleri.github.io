Ethical Principles

When scientists in disciplines such as biology, biochemistry, or biomedicine encounter the term ethics, they often associate it with procedural requirements, like GDPR compliance, ethics review boards, consent forms, animal welfare regulations, or laboratory safety standards. While bioethicists do indeed participate in such oversight processes, evaluating whether consent forms adequately communicate risks or whether animal use in research is justified, their role extends further. As philosophers, ethicists also engage with deeper questions: What does it mean to claim that animals are not “unnecessarily harmed”? How do we define “harm,” and under what conditions is it considered acceptable? How should we weigh human interests against those of other-than-human animals?

Ethical reflection need not be the sole responsibility of ethicists; scientists themselves can actively engage with the ethical dimensions of their research from the early stages of design. By identifying and considering the values embedded in their protocols, they can make informed adjustments that align with ethical principles. Below, we offer a few examples as starting points. These principles are not exhaustive and can be adapted, modified, or supplemented according to the specific context and needs of your research.

Note: The principles outlined below are intended to serve as foundational elements in the ethical deliberation of any research design. While researchers have the freedom to include, exclude, or incorporate additional ethical principles based on their project's specific context, it is strongly recommended that the following principles be considered by default. Doing so helps establish a justice-oriented approach and reduces the risk of overlooking important ethical concerns. If any of these principles are deemed inapplicable, it is advisable to document the reasons for their exclusion to ensure transparency and accountability in the research process.

1. Beneficence (doing good)

Beauchamp and Childress define the principle of beneficence as requiring one to act by keeping the best interests of the other in mind. It is a positive duty that can be applied to an individual or society. Applying this principle in SynBio would mean that researchers and companies aim for outcomes that promote human and environmental well-being.

Example: Engineered Microbes to Reduce Chemical Fertilizer Use

A synthetic biology research team develops engineered soil microbes capable of fixing atmospheric nitrogen more efficiently, aiming to reduce the reliance on synthetic nitrogen fertilizers in agriculture. These microbes are designed to form symbiotic relationships with a variety of crop plants, thereby enhancing crop yields and improving soil health while significantly lowering the environmental impact associated with conventional fertilizer use. This application exemplifies the principle of beneficence in multiple ways. First, it promotes well-being by enhancing food security and supporting the livelihoods of small-scale farmers through reduced input costs and increased productivity. Second, it minimizes harm by decreasing the environmental damage caused by chemical fertilizers, such as water pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, and soil degradation—thereby contributing to broader public and ecological health. Finally, the project incorporates equity considerations by actively involving local farming communities in the research process, ensuring that the benefits of the technology are accessible, context-specific, and do not disproportionately advantage large agribusinesses.

2. Non-maleficence (avoiding harm)

Nonmaleficence is defined by Beauchamp and Childress as the principle of causing no harm. It is the ethical duty to avoid causing harm. Nonmaleficence in synthetic biology requires that research and innovation avoid causing significant harm to humans, more-than-human life, or ecosystems. This principle calls for careful risk assessment and the implementation of safety measures throughout the research process. Projects should be designed to minimize potential negative consequences, such as unintended ecological disruptions, biosecurity threats, or adverse health effects, thereby ensuring that the pursuit of scientific advancement does not come at the expense of environmental or social well-being.

Example: Containment Strategies for Engineered Organisms

An example of applying the principle of nonmaleficence in synthetic biology can be seen in efforts to engineer bacteria capable of degrading plastic waste in marine environments. While such organisms have the potential to address significant environmental challenges, their release into open ecosystems could carry serious risks, including disruption of marine microbial communities or unintended gene transfer to wild species. To prevent such harm, researchers implement robust biocontainment strategies, such as genetic kill switches that ensure the bacteria cannot survive outside controlled conditions, and environmental safeguards that restrict their viability to specific settings. Additionally, thorough laboratory testing and ecological risk assessments are conducted before any field application. These measures reflect a commitment to minimizing harm while advancing scientific solutions, in alignment with the ethical principle of nonmaleficence. By proactively minimizing ecological risks and preventing unintended harm, the project upholds the ethical obligation to “do no harm” while still pursuing beneficial outcomes.

3. Justice (fair distribution of benefits and burdens)

Beauchamp and Childress define justice as the principle that requires a fair distribution of benefits, risks and harms. Ethically, it refers to the obligation to treat individuals and groups fairly, equitably, and impartially. In bioethics and research ethics, this principle often guides decisions about who receives access to healthcare, who participates in research, and how the burdens and benefits of scientific advancement are shared across society.

Example: Equitable access to gene-edited crops

An example of applying the principle of justice in synthetic biology can be seen in the development of drought-resistant, gene-edited crops designed to improve food security in regions heavily impacted by climate change. To ensure that the benefits of this innovation do not disproportionately favor wealthy nations or large agribusinesses, the research team partners with local governments, smallholder farmer cooperatives, and non-profit organizations in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. The project adopts open-access or low-cost licensing models to make the technology accessible to small-scale farmers, while also involving local communities in the design and testing phases to ensure their needs and knowledge inform the development process. In addition, investments are made in training and infrastructure to build local capacity and support the autonomous use of the technology. Profits or benefits generated are reinvested in the communities where the crops are used, promoting equitable benefit-sharing. Through these measures, the project actively addresses structural inequalities and exemplifies a commitment to justice in the global application of synthetic biology.

4. Respect for Autonomy

Beauchamp and Childress emphasize the importance of respecting an individual's capacity to make their own decisions about their healthcare, based on their values and beliefs. The philosophical underpinning for autonomy, accepted as an ethical principle, is that all persons have intrinsic and unconditional worth, and therefore, should have the power to make rational decisions and moral choices, and each should be allowed to exercise his or her capacity for self-determination. In the SynBio context, this means ensuring that all stakeholders, including research participants, local communities, and end-users, are adequately informed about the nature, purpose, risks, and implications of a project, and are allowed to provide free and informed consent. Respect for autonomy also includes acknowledging and incorporating diverse cultural values, knowledge systems, and ethical frameworks into research design and implementation

Example: Community Consent for Field Trials of Gene-Edited Mosquitoes

A synthetic biology project involves the release of gene-edited mosquitoes designed to suppress populations of disease-carrying species, such as Aedes aegypti, to reduce the spread of diseases like dengue or malaria. Before conducting field trials in a rural region where these diseases are prevalent, the research team engages extensively with local communities. To uphold the principle of respect for autonomy, the project prioritizes transparent communication and community engagement. Researchers provide accessible information about the technology, including its purpose, how it works, potential benefits, and risks. They organize community meetings, consult with local leaders, and allow time for questions, discussion, and deliberation. Participation in the field trial area is made contingent upon obtaining free, prior, and informed consent from the community, ensuring that individuals and groups have the freedom to make decisions without coercion or misinformation. Moreover, mechanisms are put in place for ongoing dialogue, feedback, and the ability to withdraw consent if concerns arise. This approach demonstrates how respecting autonomy in synthetic biology involves more than individual consent; it also means acknowledging collective decision-making rights, especially when research affects shared environments or community health.

5. Precautionary Principle

The precautionary principle enables decision-makers to adopt precautionary measures when scientific evidence about an environmental or human health hazard is uncertain, and the stakes are high. The precautionary principle in synthetic biology emphasizes the need to act with caution in the face of scientific uncertainty, especially when proposed innovations carry the potential for serious or irreversible harm to human health, biodiversity, or ecosystems. It holds that the absence of full scientific certainty should not be used as a reason to delay protective measures when there is a plausible risk. In the context of synthetic biology, where researchers design and construct novel organisms or modify existing ones in unprecedented ways, the principle is particularly relevant due to the unpredictability of ecological interactions, gene flow, or long-term consequences of releasing engineered organisms into the environment.

Example: Delaying the release of gene drive mosquitoes into the wild until thorough ecological risk assessments are completed.

A research team develops a gene drive, a genetic engineering technology designed to rapidly spread a particular trait through a population, to control invasive mosquito species in an island ecosystem. While the goal is to protect the people living there, the long-term ecological consequences of releasing a gene drive into the wild are uncertain, particularly regarding unintended spread to other populations or irreversible changes to the ecosystem. Following the precautionary principle, the researchers chose to delay the environmental release of the gene drive. Instead, they conduct extensive laboratory trials, ecological modeling, and controlled enclosure studies to better understand the gene drive’s behavior and potential risks. They also establish independent ethical and ecological review panels and actively engage with local communities, indigenous groups, and conservation organizations to assess social, ethical, and ecological implications. By choosing to proceed cautiously in the face of uncertainty, and only after developing robust containment, monitoring, and mitigation strategies, the research team upholds the precautionary principle. This approach helps prevent potential irreversible harm and builds public trust, ensuring that the technology is deployed responsibly, if at all.

6. Intergenerational responsibility

The principle of intergenerational equity states that every generation holds the Earth in common with members of the present generation and with other generations, past and future. The principle articulates a concept of fairness among generations in the use and conservation of the environment and its natural resources. The principle of intergenerational responsibility in synthetic biology underscores the ethical duty to consider the long-term impacts of innovations on future generations. It calls for sustainable design, robust risk assessment, and cautious deployment of engineered organisms to avoid irreversible ecological harm. SynBio should contribute to a future that is equitable, resilient, and environmentally sound

Example: Engineering coral for climate resilience

A synthetic biology project aims to enhance coral resilience to ocean warming by genetically modifying symbiotic algae that live within corals. These modified algae are designed to improve coral thermal tolerance and reduce bleaching, helping preserve reef ecosystems threatened by climate change. Before releasing the modified algae into marine environments, the research team undertakes long-term ecological modeling and closed-environment trials to assess the potential effects on marine biodiversity, food chains, and evolutionary dynamics. They consult with marine ecologists, ethicists, and local communities, and incorporate regulatory safeguards, including reversibility strategies and containment plans. By prioritizing caution and sustainability, the project upholds intergenerational responsibility, aiming to preserve coral reef ecosystems not just for current populations but for future generations who depend on these ecosystems for biodiversity, cultural heritage, and coastal protection.
