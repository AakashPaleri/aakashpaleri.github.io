Relationship with Stakeholders

Stakeholder relationships in synthetic biology refer to the complex and dynamic interactions among a broad range of actors who are either directly involved in or potentially affected by the development, deployment, and governance of synthetic biology technologies. These stakeholders include, but are not limited to, scientists, policymakers, industry leaders, regulatory bodies, ethicists, civil society organizations, Indigenous communities, local populations, end-users such as farmers and patients, and the more-than-human life participating and affected by our research. Each of these groups brings distinct perspectives, priorities, and values to the discourse surrounding SynBio, shaping its ethical, social, and environmental implications. Establishing and nurturing these relationships requires more than mere consultation; it involves sustained engagement characterized by transparency, mutual respect, and shared decision-making. In particular, ethical stakeholder engagement must prioritize inclusivity, ensuring that marginalized or vulnerable communities are meaningfully involved in shaping the research agendas and governance frameworks that may impact their lives and environments. It also necessitates the creation of mechanisms for accountability and feedback, especially in cases where synthetic biology applications intersect with public goods such as health, agriculture, or ecosystems. Moreover, given the transformative and potentially disruptive nature of synthetic biology, the cultivation of strong stakeholder relationships serves not only as an ethical imperative but also as a means to build public trust, anticipate societal concerns, and ensure that innovation is aligned with collective values and long-term sustainability goals. These relationships thus form the social infrastructure through which synthetic biology can responsibly evolve as a field, striking a balance between technological advancement, democratic legitimacy, and ecological responsibility.

Key Stakeholders in Synthetic Biology:

1. Scientists and Researchers

Role: Develop SynBio tools, models, and applications.

Scientists, including molecular biologists, bioengineers, and computational biologists, are at the core of SynBio innovation. They design, test, and refine synthetic biological systems, from genetic circuits to engineered organisms. Their decisions, methodologies, and priorities shape the trajectory of the field, influencing which problems are addressed and which populations or ecosystems are impacted. They also bear ethical responsibilities regarding biosafety, biosecurity, and dual-use concerns, often navigating complex tensions between innovation and precaution.

2. Policymakers and Regulators

Role: Create legal and governance frameworks.

Policymakers and regulatory bodies establish the legal and ethical frameworks within which SynBio operates. They are responsible for creating standards for research oversight, product approval, environmental release, intellectual property, and international trade. Effective regulation must balance innovation with public safety and environmental integrity. Policymakers also play a critical role in ensuring inclusive public engagement and accountability, particularly when technologies cross borders or affect global commons.

3. Industry and Innovators

Role: Translate SynBio research into commercial products.

Biotech companies, startups, and corporate R&D departments translate SynBio research into marketable products such as synthetic vaccines, bio-based materials, or engineered crops. These actors influence funding priorities and often set the pace of innovation. Their decisions about product design, distribution, and pricing can significantly affect accessibility, sustainability, and equity. The commercialization process may also prioritize profit over precaution, necessitating strong ethical and regulatory oversight.

4. Civil Society and NGOs

Role: Advocate for social, ethical, and environmental concerns.

These actors function as watchdogs, advocates, and mediators. Civil society organizations and NGOs raise critical questions about the societal, ethical, and ecological implications of SynBio. They often highlight the perspectives of marginalized groups and bring attention to long-term impacts such as ecological disruption, corporate monopolization, or unintended societal consequences. They also facilitate public engagement, promote transparency, and push for democratic governance of emerging biotechnologies.

5. Local Communities and Indigenous People

Role: Often directly affected by SynBio interventions.

These communities may live in regions where SynBio applications, such as genetically modified crops, gene drives, or microbial bioremediation, are implemented. They have unique relationships with land, biodiversity, and traditional ecological knowledge. SynBio interventions can affect their livelihoods, cultural practices, and autonomy. In many cases, they are not adequately consulted in decision-making processes, raising concerns about consent, justice, and epistemic colonialism. Recognizing their rights and knowledge systems is essential for ethical and inclusive SynBio development.

6. Farmers, Consumers, and End-users

Role: Engage with SynBio through food, health, and materials.

This broad group includes those who grow, use, or consume SynBio products. Farmers may adopt SynBio-derived crops, while consumers might encounter synthetic ingredients in food, cosmetics, or medicines. Their acceptance and trust are critical to the success of such technologies. However, they often lack the information or agency to influence upstream decisions. Issues such as labeling, affordability, and long-term effects on health and livelihoods are central to ethical discourse around SynBio products.

7. Ethicists and Social Scientists

Role: Analyze societal impacts and value systems.

These scholars play a key role in analyzing the broader implications of SynBio. Ethicists examine issues such as the moral status of engineered life forms, intergenerational justice, and distributive fairness. Social scientists study the socio-political contexts, power dynamics, and cultural dimensions of technological development. Together, they contribute to anticipatory governance, responsible innovation frameworks, and ethical reflection that go beyond compliance to question underlying values and assumptions in SynBio.

8. More-than-human life

Role: Includes all biological entities affected by or participating in SynBio (e.g., microbes, ecosystems, animals).

This category includes microbial entities engineered in the lab, more-than-human organisms modified or affected by SynBio, and broader ecological systems. These beings often become the substrates or hosts of human innovation without agency or representation. Including more-than-human life as stakeholders challenges anthropocentric frameworks and calls for ecological ethics and relational ontologies. It prompts questions about our responsibilities toward co-inhabitants of Earth and the kinds of futures SynBio creates for all forms of life. While ethical reflection is often associated with research involving human or animal subjects, a common misconception persists among researchers who work exclusively with only microbial life that ethics is less relevant in their context. However, this view overlooks the role of more-than-human entities in the lab work.

Being ethically engaged begins with recognizing the multiple contributions of living organisms, even those considered “background” or “invisible” in experimental systems. There are many contributions from different forms of life in SynBio research, either in the form of subjects, tools, vectors, or biochemical producers. Their roles are often taken for granted, yet they are foundational to the success of scientific outcomes.

A first step toward cultivating ethical awareness in this context is to map the more-than-human landscape of your work. This can help reveal relationships of dependency, exploitation, and care that often go unnoticed. Reflecting on this inventory helps uncover the layered web of life entangled in laboratory practices. It also opens space for new ethical questions:

  • Which organisms are involved in our experiments?
  • Which organisms are rendered visible or invisible in our work?
  • What assumptions underlie their use or manipulation?
  • How might care, reciprocity, or responsibility be extended to them?

By making these relationships explicit, researchers can begin to adopt a relational ethics of care that moves beyond human-centered concerns to include the more-than-human participants of scientific life.
