ENGINEERED HEAT-RESISTANT CORAL

Global climate change has led to a significant rise in seawater temperatures, resulting in widespread coral bleaching and the degradation of coral reef ecosystems. These reefs are vital for marine biodiversity, coastal protection, and the livelihoods of millions. Their loss signals profound ecological disruption and highlights the urgency of climate action. Synthetic Biology has a solution to this problem. Using synthetic biology, corals’ DNA can be redesigned to withstand higher temperatures, producing “heat-resistant coral”. However, this also raises some ethical concerns.

What are the ethical issues with engineered heat-resistant coral?

1. Intervention in Complex Ecosystems: Coral reefs are intricate ecological systems with tightly balanced interdependencies between species. Introducing engineered corals may disrupt symbiotic relationships in the sea (e.g., with algae or microbial communities). There is uncertainty about long-term ecological consequences, including effects on biodiversity, reef dynamics, and food webs.

2. Technolofix mentality: Similar to bioremediation, engineered corals are at a risk of erasing the possibilities of confronting the root cause: global climate change. It risks the reliance on technological solutions, while normalizing the continuation of practices harmful to nature.

3. Risk of Genetic Contamination: Engineered corals may interbreed with wild populations, spreading modified traits across natural reefs. This raises the possibility of uncontrolled genetic flow and loss of genetic diversity, which could undermine long-term adaptability.

4. Informed Consent and Inclusion of Affected Communities: Coral reefs support the livelihoods and cultural identities of many coastal and Indigenous communities. Decisions to deploy engineered corals often happen in scientific or governmental spaces, without adequate consultation with those most affected.

5. Prioritization and Funding: Large-scale coral engineering projects may divert funds and attention away from low-tech, community-led reef restoration or traditional ecological knowledge systems. This can overshadow low expertise, traditional knowledge systems that focus on more sustainable, long-term solutions.
